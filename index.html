03
The Builder

I didn't choose engineering.
Engineering chose me.

My first reaction to a problem has never been "who do I ask" — it's always been "what do I build." That instinct started before I could name it: taking apart electronics, rebuilding them wrong, then right, then better. It's still the same instinct, just running on better hardware now.

I'm studying Electrical & Electronics Engineering at Coventry University, Cairo. But the curriculum is the scaffold, not the building. The actual education has been: building a sensor-fused platform that beat every university team at ISEF, co-founding a startup that impressed Y Combinator alumni, and constructing a robot that Egypt's government decided was worth recognising at EU level.

I operate at the intersection of engineering and business — which means I can write the firmware and present the pitch deck. I understand root cause analysis because I've lived it on hardware that fails at 2am before a competition. I understand stakeholder management because I've closed sponsorship deals under pressure with a committee of 12 people counting on me.

The thesis: the most important problems don't belong to one discipline. I refuse to belong to just one either.

The project that matters most
I built a voice for my deaf cousin.

She navigates a world built for people who can hear. The problem isn't technology — it's that nobody bothered to build a bridge. So I did. Flex sensors down each finger, an IMU on the back of the hand, a trained gesture classifier, an onboard speaker.

She signs. It speaks. No app, no phone, no intermediary. Just her hands, and a voice.

"I didn't build this to win a competition. I built it because watching someone you love wait for a translator is something that should stop existing."
